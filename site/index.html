Time lapse recording features

Everything you need to record a time-lapse without touching a video editor.

Screen, webcam, or both

Record a screen time lapse, a webcam time lapse, or your screen with a draggable webcam box in the corner.

Speed presets up to 600×

From 30× up to 600×, or set a custom interval. At the default 150×, one hour becomes a 24-second clip.

Elapsed time in the video

A running clock is stamped into the footage so viewers see how long you actually worked. Optional floating timer stays out of the recording.

Pause and resume

Take a break without ending the session. Paused time does not count toward the clock.

MP4, WebM, MOV, and GIF

Export your time-lapse in four formats. Quality options from 720p up to 1440p.

Private by default

Everything runs locally on your PC and works offline. No account, no cloud upload, no analytics.

Blur sensitive areas

Draw draggable blur regions to hide passwords, messages, or other on-screen content during capture.

Auto-stop timer

Stop recording automatically after a set number of hours — useful for overnight sessions.

Lightweight on long sessions

Captures still frames instead of full video, so a three-hour session uses minimal CPU and memory.

How time lapse recording works

  1. Press record

    Choose screen, webcam, or both. Pick your speed preset and hit Start Recording.

  2. Work as usual

    LapseCam quietly saves one JPEG every few seconds instead of recording constant video — even multi-hour sessions stay light on your machine.

  3. Press stop

    ffmpeg stitches the frames into your finished time-lapse. The video lands in your Videos folder within seconds.

Built for speed and privacy

I built LapseCam in plain JavaScript on Electron — no UI framework, no bloat. Most time lapse tools record full video and speed it up afterward, which eats disk space and CPU. LapseCam draws the screen and webcam onto a canvas and saves one JPEG every few seconds. A three-hour session at the default speed is only around two thousand small photos on disk.

When you press stop, a bundled ffmpeg binary stitches the photos into the final video and streams progress back to the UI. The renderer never touches Node — file writes, encoding, and dialogs go through a context-isolated preload bridge. electron-builder packages everything into an installer and a portable exe.

Frequently asked questions

What is LapseCam?

LapseCam is a free, open source time lapse recording tool for Windows. It captures your screen, webcam, or both and automatically produces a sped-up video when you stop — no video editor required.

Is LapseCam actually free?

Yes. Free and open source under the MIT license. The tip button is optional, not a paywall.

How is this different from OBS or a screen recorder?

Screen recorders capture full video in real time. LapseCam captures still frames at intervals and stitches them into a time-lapse on stop. That means multi-hour sessions use very little disk space and CPU compared to recording and then speeding up footage in an editor.

What speed should I use for a study time lapse?

The default 150× works well for most study sessions — one hour becomes about 24 seconds. For very long sessions (4+ hours), try 300× or 600×. You can also set a custom frame interval.

Windows says it "protected your PC". Is something wrong?

No. The exe is not code signed yet (signing certificates cost hundreds per year). Click "More info", then "Run anyway". The full source code is public if you prefer to build it yourself.

Does it record audio?

No. Time-lapses are silent, so LapseCam never asks for your microphone.

Where do my time-lapse videos end up?

In Videos\LapseCam by default. You can pick a different folder in Settings.

Is there a Mac or Linux version?

Not yet. LapseCam is built and tested on Windows 10 and 11. The code is open source if you want to port it.

Can I use LapseCam offline?

Yes. Recording and encoding work fully offline. Only the optional update check needs internet.
